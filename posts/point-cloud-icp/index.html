What is a point cloud?

Forget polygons. Forget smooth surfaces. A point cloud is the rawest way a machine can see the world: a bag of 3D coordinates. Each point is just an (x, y, z) position in space — nothing more.

A LiDAR or depth camera doesn't see surfaces. It sees samples — individual points scattered across whatever it can reach.

Think of it like this: you're in a pitch-black room with a laser pointer. Every time the laser hits a wall, you record that position. After a thousand hits, you have a point cloud — a dotted outline of the room.

No triangles. No edges. No connectivity. Just dots in space. That's both the beauty and the challenge.

Where do point clouds come from?

📡
LiDAR
Shoots laser pulses and measures return time. Millions of points per second. Used in self-driving cars, surveying, archaeology.
📷
Depth Cameras
Intel RealSense, Kinect, iPhone TrueDepth. Structured light or time-of-flight. Dense, short-range.
🛰️
Photogrammetry
Multiple photos → triangulated 3D points. Works from drones, phones, satellites. Structure-from-Motion (SfM).
Key insight

All these sensors share a problem: one scan is never enough. A LiDAR on a car sees the front of a building, not the back. A depth camera captures one angle at a time. To build a complete 3D model, you need to stitch multiple scans together.

This is the registration problem — and it's where ICP enters the story.

The alignment problem

Imagine you scan a room from two positions. Each scan lives in its own coordinate system. They overlap, but they don't line up.

Your job: find a rotation and translation that moves Scan B on top of Scan A so the overlapping regions match up.

This is called rigid registration — "rigid" because we're not squishing or stretching. We're only rotating and sliding.

ICP: The intuition

The Iterative Closest Point algorithm (Besl & McKay, 1992) solves registration with a beautifully simple idea:

The ICP idea in one sentence

Pair each point with its nearest neighbor, then nudge the whole cloud to shrink those pairings. Repeat until nothing moves.

It's like a dance. Two groups of people stand in a room. Each person in Group B finds the nearest person in Group A and takes one small step toward them. Then everyone re-evaluates who's closest and steps again. After enough rounds, the groups overlap.

ICP: Step by step

Find closest points

For every point in the source cloud, find its nearest neighbor in the target cloud. This gives you a set of pairs. In practice, this uses a KD-tree for speed — turning an O(n²) search into O(n log n).

Compute the best rigid transform

Given those pairs, compute the rotation R and translation t that minimizes the sum of squared distances between paired points. This has a beautiful closed-form solution using SVD (Singular Value Decomposition).

Apply the transform

Move every source point by the computed R and t. The source cloud takes one step closer to the target.

Check convergence

Measure the total error (sum of distances between pairs). If it barely changed since last iteration — or you've hit a max iteration count — stop. Otherwise, go back to Step 1.

Why "iterative"?

The pairings from Step 1 are wrong at first. Point A might pair with the wrong neighbor because the clouds aren't aligned yet. But that's fine — the pairs are "good enough" to nudge the cloud in the right direction. As the cloud moves, the pairings improve. Better pairings → better transform → better pairings. This virtuous cycle is the engine of ICP.

The math (gently)

Let's peek under the hood. Don't panic — we'll build the equations piece by piece, and every symbol maps to something you can see.

What we're minimizing

Given source points pᵢ and their closest target points qᵢ, we want:

minimize E(R, t) = Σᵢ ‖ R·pᵢ + t − qᵢ ‖²

In words: rotate each source point, shift it, then measure how far it is from its paired target point. Sum all those squared distances. Find the R and t that make this sum as small as possible.

Step-by-step solution

1. Center both clouds.

Compute the centroid (average position) of each cloud. Subtract it so both clouds sit at the origin. This separates the rotation problem from translation.

p̄ = (1/n) Σ pᵢ q̄ = (1/n) Σ qᵢ

2. Build the cross-covariance matrix H.

H = Σᵢ (pᵢ − p̄)(qᵢ − q̄)ᵀ

This 3×3 matrix encodes how the two point clouds co-vary. It captures the "twist" between them.

3. SVD to extract the rotation.

H = U · Σ · Vᵀ → R = V · Uᵀ

The SVD splits H into its fundamental rotation components. The optimal rotation is simply V times U-transpose.

4. Recover translation.

t = q̄ − R · p̄

Once you know the rotation, translation falls out immediately: it's just the difference between the rotated source centroid and the target centroid.

The beautiful thing

Each iteration solves a least-squares problem exactly. The approximation only comes from the pairing step — we don't know the true correspondences, so we guess using nearest neighbors. But each guess gets better, and the whole thing converges.

Where ICP struggles

✓ ICP works well when

  • Initial alignment is close (< 30° rotation)
  • High overlap between scans
  • Low noise
  • Dense sampling

✗ ICP struggles when

  • Clouds are far apart (local minimum trap)
  • Low overlap — wrong correspondences dominate
  • Symmetric objects — multiple valid alignments
  • Outliers and noise distort the transform

Beyond basic ICP

Researchers have spent 30+ years improving ICP. Some key variants:

Point-to-Plane ICP

Instead of minimizing point-to-point distance, minimize the distance from each source point to the tangent plane at the target point. Converges much faster on smooth surfaces.

Trimmed ICP

Only use the best k% of correspondences. This rejects outliers and handles partial overlap gracefully.

Generalized ICP (GICP)

Models the local surface structure as a covariance matrix at each point. Unifies point-to-point and point-to-plane as special cases.

Global registration (RANSAC, FGR, TEASER++)

These skip closest-point pairing entirely. They use feature descriptors (like FPFH) to find correspondences across large displacements, then refine with ICP. This solves the "bad starting position" problem.

The bigger picture

ICP is the "hello world" of point cloud alignment — and it's still used in production every day. SLAM systems in autonomous vehicles, AR headsets, and robots all use ICP (or its variants) as a core building block. Understanding ICP gives you a foundation for understanding everything from LiDAR odometry to 3D reconstruction pipelines.
