The problem: needles in triangle haystacks

You have a 3D model — say a character mesh with 80,000 triangles. A user clicks the screen. You need to answer: which triangle did they click on?

The naive approach: test the click-ray against every single triangle. 80,000 ray-triangle intersection tests. Every frame. At 60fps, that's 4.8 million tests per second — for a single ray.

Now imagine a game with selection highlights, physics raycasts for ground detection, visibility checks, shadow rays, mouse hover effects... You might need hundreds of rays per frame. The naive approach collapses instantly.

THE CORE QUESTION

How do you avoid testing a ray against triangles it could never possibly hit?

The answer is the same trick humans use when searching a library. You don't check every book on every shelf. You go to the right floor, the right section, the right shelf, then scan individual spines. You spatially narrow your search.

A Bounding Volume Hierarchy (BVH) does exactly this for 3D geometry.

What is a BVH?

A BVH is a binary tree where:

Every leaf holds triangles

The bottom of the tree stores small groups of actual triangles — typically ≤10 per leaf.

Every node holds a bounding box

Each node stores an axis-aligned bounding box (AABB) that completely encloses all triangles beneath it.

The root encloses the entire mesh

The topmost box wraps every triangle. Its children split that space into two roughly-equal halves. Their children split further. And so on.

Think of it like nested boxes. The biggest box contains two smaller boxes. Each of those contains two even smaller boxes. Keep going until the boxes are small enough to hold just a few triangles.

KEY INSIGHT

If a ray misses a bounding box, it misses everything inside that box. One cheap ray-box test eliminates thousands of expensive ray-triangle tests.

For a mesh with N triangles, brute force costs O(N) per ray. A BVH reduces this to O(log N) — the depth of the tree. For 80,000 triangles, that's roughly 17 box tests instead of 80,000 triangle tests.

O(N) → O(log N) · For 80K triangles: 80,000 tests → ~17 tests

That's a 4,700× reduction. The difference between a slideshow and smooth real-time interaction.

Why axis-aligned boxes?

An AABB (Axis-Aligned Bounding Box) is defined by just 6 numbers: min and max along each of X, Y, Z. It's the tightest rectangular box whose faces are parallel to the coordinate axes. Why not spheres, or oriented boxes?

  • Storage: 6 floats per node. Oriented boxes need 15+ (center, axes, half-extents).
  • Ray test: The AABB ray test (slab method) uses only subtractions, multiplications, and min/max — no square roots, no dot products. About 13 operations total.
  • Construction: Computing the AABB of N points is O(N) — just track min/max per axis.
  • Merging: The AABB enclosing two AABBs is computed with 6 min/max operations.

The downside: AABBs can waste space on diagonal geometry (imagine a long thin rod tilted at 45°). But in practice, the simplicity and speed of AABB tests overwhelm the slightly-looser fits, especially when you have many levels of hierarchy.

Split strategies — choosing where to cut

How do you decide which triangles go in the left child vs. the right child? This is the split strategy, and it dramatically affects performance. three-mesh-bvh offers three:

CENTER

Find the longest axis. Split at the spatial midpoint of the bounding box. Triangles whose centroids fall below the midpoint go left, the rest go right.

⚡ Fastest to build (no sorting needed)
✗ Can produce unbalanced trees when geometry is clustered
✗ Bad for non-uniform distributions

AVERAGE

Split at the average centroid position along the longest axis. Adapts to where the triangles actually are, not where the box center is.

⚡ Slightly slower (must compute mean)
✓ Better for clustered geometry
✗ Still purely geometric — ignores query cost

But the real star is the Surface Area Heuristic (SAH).

The Surface Area Heuristic

SAH asks: "If a random ray passes through the parent box, what's the expected cost of this split?" The key insight comes from geometric probability:

SAH PRINCIPLE

The probability that a random ray hits a child box is proportional to the child's surface area relative to the parent's. A child with half the parent's surface area will be hit by roughly half the rays.

Why surface area? Imagine standing outside a box and throwing darts randomly at it from all directions. The chance of hitting it is proportional to how much "target" it presents — its surface area. A long thin box has less surface area than a big cube, so random rays are less likely to hit it.

The cost of a split is:

Cost = C_trav + (SA_left / SA_parent) × N_left × C_isect + (SA_right / SA_parent) × N_right × C_isect

Where:

  • SA_left, SA_right = surface areas of the two child boxes
  • SA_parent = surface area of the parent box
  • N_left, N_right = number of triangles in each child
  • C_trav = cost of testing a ray against one box (~1 unit)
  • C_isect = cost of testing a ray against one triangle (~8 units)

In practice, three-mesh-bvh uses a binned SAH approximation: instead of testing every possible split position, it divides each axis into 12 buckets, counts how many triangle centroids fall into each bucket, and evaluates the SAH cost at each bucket boundary. This reduces the O(N²) exhaustive search to O(N) per axis — fast enough to build million-triangle BVHs.

BINNED SAH — Step by Step

for each axis in [X, Y, Z]:
    1. Compute centroid bounds along this axis
    2. Divide into 12 equal-width buckets
    3. For each triangle: assign to bucket by centroid position
    4. For each bucket: accumulate (count, bbox)
    5. Sweep left-to-right: compute prefix sums of (count, bbox)
    6. Sweep right-to-left: compute suffix sums of (count, bbox)
    7. For each split position (11 candidates):
         cost = SA(left_bbox) × left_count + SA(right_bbox) × right_count
    8. Track minimum cost across all axes and positions

Total: 3 axes × 12 buckets × N assignments = O(N) work

Intuitively: SAH avoids splits that create one huge box and one tiny box. It prefers splits where both children have small surface areas — because smaller boxes are less likely to be hit by random rays. It also avoids putting too many triangles on one side.

✗

Bad split

Left child: 95% of the surface area, 60% of the triangles. Most rays still have to visit it. Barely faster than no split at all.

✓

Good split (SAH)

Left child: 40% surface area, 45% triangles. Right child: 50% surface area, 55% triangles. Rays are routed efficiently to smaller subproblems.

Construction pseudocode

function buildBVH(triangles, depth):
    // Base case: few enough triangles for a leaf
    if triangles.length ≤ maxLeafTris:
        return LeafNode(boundingBox(triangles), triangles)

    // Try splitting along each axis (X, Y, Z)
    bestCost = Infinity
    for axis in [X, Y, Z]:
        // Divide axis range into 12 buckets
        for each bucket boundary:
            left, right = partition(triangles, axis, boundary)
            cost = SAH_cost(left, right, triangles)
            if cost < bestCost:
                bestCost = cost
                bestSplit = (axis, boundary)

    // If splitting isn't worth it, make a leaf
    if bestCost > N_triangles × C_intersect:
        return LeafNode(boundingBox(triangles), triangles)

    // Recurse
    left, right = partition(triangles, bestSplit)
    return InternalNode(
        box:   boundingBox(triangles),
        left:  buildBVH(left,  depth + 1),
        right: buildBVH(right, depth + 1)
    )

The defaults in three-mesh-bvh: maxLeafTris = 10, maxDepth = 40, strategy = SAH (since v0.5). For an 80K triangle mesh, the tree typically has ~15 levels and ~16,000 nodes.

The ray-AABB slab test

The BVH's power depends on testing "does this ray hit this box?" being very cheap. The standard algorithm is the slab method, invented by Kay & Kajiya in 1986. It's elegant and branchless.

The idea: slabs

Think of an AABB as the intersection of 3 "slabs" — one per axis. A slab is the space between two parallel planes. The X-slab is everything between x=min_x and x=max_x. The Y-slab is between y=min_y and y=max_y. The Z-slab is between z=min_z and z=max_z.

A point is inside the box if and only if it's inside all three slabs simultaneously.

For a ray, we compute when it enters and exits each slab:

RAY-AABB SLAB TEST

// Ray: P(t) = origin + t × direction
// Box: [min_x, max_x] × [min_y, max_y] × [min_z, max_z]

// For each axis, find t-values where ray crosses the slab boundaries
t_x_enter = (min_x - origin.x) / direction.x
t_x_exit  = (max_x - origin.x) / direction.x
if t_x_enter > t_x_exit: swap(t_x_enter, t_x_exit)  // handle negative direction

t_y_enter = (min_y - origin.y) / direction.y
t_y_exit  = (max_y - origin.y) / direction.y
if t_y_enter > t_y_exit: swap(t_y_enter, t_y_exit)

t_z_enter = (min_z - origin.z) / direction.z
t_z_exit  = (max_z - origin.z) / direction.z
if t_z_enter > t_z_exit: swap(t_z_enter, t_z_exit)

// The ray is inside the box when it's inside ALL slabs simultaneously
t_enter = max(t_x_enter, t_y_enter, t_z_enter)  // latest entry
t_exit  = min(t_x_exit,  t_y_exit,  t_z_exit)   // earliest exit

// Hit if: entered before exited, AND exit is in front of ray origin
return t_enter ≤ t_exit AND t_exit ≥ 0

Why is this so fast? Only 6 subtractions, 6 multiplications, 2 swaps (or min/max), 2 three-way max/min, and 2 comparisons. No branches needed — modern CPUs can compute this entirely with SIMD instructions. The GPU does it in a single clock cycle.

GEOMETRIC INTUITION

Imagine shining a laser through 3 sets of venetian blinds at right angles. The laser only reaches the far wall if it passes through the gap in ALL THREE sets of blinds at the same time. t_enter is when the laser first passes through all three gaps simultaneously. t_exit is when it leaves any one of them.

Traversal — skipping what you can't hit

Once the tree is built, querying it is elegant: 

function raycast(ray, node):
    // Does the ray hit this node's bounding box?
    if not rayIntersectsAABB(ray, node.box):
        return null     // Skip this ENTIRE subtree!

    // Leaf node: test actual triangles
    if node.isLeaf:
        return closestHit(ray, node.triangles)

    // Internal node: recurse into both children
    hitLeft  = raycast(ray, node.left)
    hitRight = raycast(ray, node.right)
    return closer(hitLeft, hitRight)

The magic is in line 3: one cheap ray-AABB test eliminates an entire subtree. At depth 3, skipping one node skips 1/8th of all triangles. At depth 5, one skip eliminates 1/32nd.

A well-built BVH means most rays touch only 15–30 nodes total out of thousands — visiting a narrow "path" through the tree from root to the one or two leaves that actually matter.

The firstHitOnly optimization

three-mesh-bvh supports raycaster.firstHitOnly = true. Instead of finding the closest hit (which requires checking all potential hits), it stops at the first confirmed hit. This enables:

  • Shadow rays — just need to know if something blocks the light
  • Occlusion tests — is point A visible from point B?
  • Ground detection — is there a floor below the player?

With firstHitOnly, the tree uses front-to-back ordering: visit the nearer child first. Once it finds a hit, it prunes the far child if that child's box is entirely behind the hit distance. This can be 2–5× faster.

FRONT-TO-BACK TRAVERSAL (firstHitOnly)

function raycastFirst(ray, node):
    if not rayIntersectsAABB(ray, node.box):
        return null

    if node.isLeaf:
        return closestHit(ray, node.triangles)

    // Determine which child's box is nearer along ray
    t_left  = rayBoxEntry(ray, node.left.box)
    t_right = rayBoxEntry(ray, node.right.box)

    // Visit nearer child first
    [near, far] = t_left < t_right ? [left, right] : [right, left]

    hit = raycastFirst(ray, near)
    if hit:
        // If far child's entry is beyond our hit, skip it entirely!
        if rayBoxEntry(ray, far.box) > hit.distance:
            return hit
    // Otherwise, check far child too
    farHit = raycastFirst(ray, far)
    return closer(hit, farHit)

Memory layout — flat arrays, not objects

A naive BVH implementation would use JavaScript objects: 

// ❌ Naive: heap-allocated objects, scattered in memory
class BVHNode {
    constructor() {
        this.boundingBox = { min: {x,y,z}, max: {x,y,z} };
        this.left = null;   // pointer to child
        this.right = null;  // pointer to child
        this.triangles = [];
    }
}

For an 80K-triangle mesh, that's ~16,000 node objects, each with nested objects for bounding boxes, plus arrays for triangle indices. That's 100,000+ heap allocations — terrible for the garbage collector, terrible for cache locality (objects scattered randomly in memory).

three-mesh-bvh does something radically different: it packs the entire tree into two flat typed arrays.

THE FLAT ARRAY TRICK

The BVH is stored as a single Float32Array for bounds data and a Uint32Array for tree structure. Zero heap allocations per node. The entire tree is one contiguous memory block — maximally cache-friendly, transferable to WebWorkers with zero-copy, and GC-invisible.

The interpretation depends on whether it's a leaf or internal node:

Internal Node

offset = byte offset to the right child
Left child is always the next node in memory (implicit)
splitAxis = 0, 1, or 2 (X, Y, Z)

Children are found without pointer chasing — just array index math.

Leaf Node

offset = start index into the (reordered) index buffer
count = number of triangles in this leaf
Leaf flag = special bit in the metadata field

Triangle data lives in the geometry's own index buffer — no duplication.

Why this matters

Cache locality

Traversal reads nodes sequentially or via predictable jumps. The CPU prefetcher loves this. A pointer-based tree causes random cache misses on every node visit.

Zero-copy WebWorker transfer

Float32Array and Uint32Array are transferable. You can send the entire BVH to a worker in microseconds — the buffer moves, not copies. Essential for off-main-thread construction.

Serialization is free

Saving/loading a BVH is just writing/reading the raw typed array bytes. No serialization logic needed. This is how MeshBVH.serialize() works — it just hands you the buffers.

GPU texture encoding

For GPU path tracing, the flat array maps directly into a texture. The fragment shader reads BVH nodes as texel fetches — same layout, no conversion needed.

Index buffer reordering

Here's a subtle but important optimization: during BVH construction, three-mesh-bvh reorders the geometry's index buffer so that triangles belonging to the same leaf are contiguous in memory. 

// Before BVH construction:
// index buffer: [tri_0, tri_1, tri_2, ..., tri_N]  (arbitrary order)

// After BVH construction:
// index buffer: [leaf_0_tris..., leaf_1_tris..., leaf_2_tris..., ...]
// Each leaf just stores: startIndex, count
// Triangles for leaf 5 are at: indexBuffer[leaf5.start .. leaf5.start + leaf5.count]

This means reading all triangles in a leaf is a single contiguous memory access. No index indirection, no scattered reads. When the GPU renders this mesh, triangles that are spatially close are also close in the vertex buffer — improving vertex cache utilization.

THE INDIRECT OPTION

If you don't want the index buffer modified (e.g., geometry has multiple draw groups), set indirect: true. This creates a separate indirection array instead of reordering the index buffer. Slightly slower traversal, but preserves the original buffer structure.

Beyond rays — spatial queries

A BVH isn't just for rays. Any spatial query expressible as "does shape X overlap box Y?" can reuse the same tree traversal. three-mesh-bvh provides a rich set of queries:

📏

closestPointToPoint

Find the nearest point on the mesh surface to a given position. Uses distance-guided pruning — if the nearest candidate so far is distance d, skip any box further than d. Essential for SDF generation and snapping.

🔮

intersectsSphere / intersectsBox

Does a sphere or box overlap any triangle? Used for area-of-effect damage, brush tools, frustum culling, region queries. Returns all affected triangles.

🏗️

shapecast

The swiss army knife. Sweep any arbitrary shape through the BVH. You provide two callbacks: one to test against bounding boxes, one to test against triangles. The BVH handles the traversal.

🎯

bvhcast (BVH vs. BVH)

Find all intersecting triangle pairs between two meshes. Both BVHs are traversed simultaneously — "dual tree traversal" — pruning entire branches of both trees at once.

📐

closestPointToGeometry

Nearest distance between two arbitrary meshes. BVH on both sides makes this tractable for complex geometry. Used in collision detection without full contact manifolds.

🗺️

refit

When mesh vertices move (animation, deformation), refit the bounding boxes bottom-up without rebuilding the whole tree. Much faster than reconstruction for small deformations.

The shapecast API

The shapecast API is the most general query. You provide two callbacks: 

bvh.shapecast({
  // Called for each BVH node: should the shape test this box?
  intersectsBounds: (box, isLeaf, score, depth) => {
    if (myShape.intersectsBox(box)) return INTERSECTED;
    return NOT_INTERSECTED;  // prune this subtree
  },

  // Called for each triangle inside a visited leaf
  intersectsTriangle: (triangle, triIndex, contained, depth) => {
    if (myShape.containsTriangle(triangle)) {
      results.push(triIndex);
    }
    return false;  // return true to stop early
  }
});

This is how three-bvh-csg implements boolean operations (union, subtraction, intersection) on meshes — shapecast one mesh's BVH against another's to find all intersecting triangle pairs, then compute the boolean.

closestPointToPoint — distance-guided pruning

Finding the closest point on a mesh to a given position is one of the most useful spatial queries. The algorithm uses a shrinking search radius:

CLOSEST POINT QUERY

function closestPointToPoint(point, node, bestDist = Infinity):
    // Can this node possibly contain a closer point?
    boxDist = distanceToBox(point, node.box)
    if boxDist > bestDist:
        return bestDist  // PRUNE: entire subtree is too far

    if node.isLeaf:
        for each triangle in node.triangles:
            d = distanceToTriangle(point, triangle)
            if d < bestDist:
                bestDist = d
                bestPoint = closestPointOnTriangle(point, triangle)
        return bestDist

    // Visit nearer child first (likely to shrink bestDist faster)
    d_left  = distanceToBox(point, node.left.box)
    d_right = distanceToBox(point, node.right.box)
    [near, far] = d_left < d_right ? [left, right] : [right, left]

    bestDist = closestPointToPoint(point, near, bestDist)
    bestDist = closestPointToPoint(point, far, bestDist)  // often pruned!
    return bestDist

The key insight: every time we find a closer triangle, our search radius shrinks. Nodes further than the current best are pruned. Visiting the nearer child first maximizes early pruning — often the far child is skipped entirely. For a well-built BVH, this takes O(log N) time instead of O(N).

Dual-tree traversal (bvhcast)

What if you need to find all intersecting triangles between two meshes? For collision detection, CSG operations, or contact manifold computation, you need every pair of triangles (one from mesh A, one from mesh B) that overlap.

Brute force: N × M triangle-triangle tests. For two 50K-triangle meshes, that's 2.5 billion tests. Impossible in real-time.

With a BVH on each mesh, you can do dual-tree traversal: traverse both trees simultaneously, pruning pairs of subtrees whose bounding boxes don't overlap.

DUAL-TREE TRAVERSAL (bvhcast)

function bvhcast(nodeA, nodeB):
    // Do these two nodes' boxes overlap?
    if not overlaps(nodeA.box, nodeB.box):
        return  // PRUNE: no triangle in A can intersect any in B

    // Both are leaves: test triangle pairs directly
    if nodeA.isLeaf AND nodeB.isLeaf:
        for triA in nodeA.triangles:
            for triB in nodeB.triangles:
                if intersects(triA, triB):
                    results.push(triA, triB)
        return

    // Descend into the LARGER node (keeps the recursion balanced)
    if nodeA.volume > nodeB.volume and not nodeA.isLeaf:
        bvhcast(nodeA.left, nodeB)
        bvhcast(nodeA.right, nodeB)
    else if not nodeB.isLeaf:
        bvhcast(nodeA, nodeB.left)
        bvhcast(nodeA, nodeB.right)
    else:
        // One is a leaf, split the other
        bvhcast(nodeA.left, nodeB)
        bvhcast(nodeA.right, nodeB)

The magic: at each step, one box-box overlap test can eliminate the entire cross-product of two subtrees. If nodeA contains 1000 triangles and nodeB contains 800, a single "no overlap" result prunes 800,000 potential pair tests.

WHY SPLIT THE LARGER NODE?

Always descending into the larger (by volume) node keeps the recursion balanced. If we always split the same tree, we'd test tiny leaves of A against the full tree of B — inefficient. Alternating based on size ensures both trees are being narrowed down simultaneously.

In three-mesh-bvh, this is exposed via the bvhcast function: 

import { bvhcast } from 'three-mesh-bvh';

const intersectingPairs = [];
bvhcast(meshA, meshB, {
  intersectsTriangles: (triA, triB, indexA, indexB) => {
    intersectingPairs.push({ indexA, indexB });
  }
});
// intersectingPairs now contains all overlapping triangle pairs

This powers three-bvh-csg (boolean mesh operations) and collision detection for non-convex meshes.

What makes it special compared to a textbook BVH?

1

Drop-in replacement

Monkey-patches Three.js's existing mesh.raycast(). Your existing code works without changes — just faster. No API migration.

Flat typed-array storage

The tree is stored in Float32Array + Uint32Array. Cache-friendly, GC-friendly, and transferable to WebWorkers with zero-copy.

WebWorker construction

Building a BVH for a 500K-triangle mesh can take 200ms+. The library offloads this to a worker thread so it doesn't freeze the UI.

Index buffer reordering

Reorders the geometry's index buffer so that triangles in the same leaf are contiguous in memory. Improves GPU vertex cache utilization.

The BVH can be serialized into textures and traversed in GLSL fragment shaders. Powers three-gpu-pathtracer.

Using it in 5 minutes

Three lines of setup. One line per mesh. That's it.

import * as THREE from 'three';
import {
  computeBoundsTree, disposeBoundsTree, acceleratedRaycast
} from 'three-mesh-bvh';

// Patch Three.js prototypes (once, at app startup)
THREE.BufferGeometry.prototype.computeBoundsTree = computeBoundsTree;
THREE.BufferGeometry.prototype.disposeBoundsTree = disposeBoundsTree;
THREE.Mesh.prototype.raycast = acceleratedRaycast;

// Build the BVH for any mesh
const geometry = new THREE.TorusKnotGeometry(10, 3, 400, 100);
const mesh = new THREE.Mesh(geometry, material);
geometry.computeBoundsTree();  // ← this line does the magic

// Raycasting is now ~100x faster. Nothing else changes.
const raycaster = new THREE.Raycaster();
raycaster.firstHitOnly = true;   // even faster for shadow/occlusion
const hits = raycaster.intersectObjects([mesh]);

For advanced queries, access the BVH directly:

const bvh = geometry.boundsTree;

// Find the closest point on the mesh to a world position
const target = {};
bvh.closestPointToPoint(point, target);
// target.point, target.distance, target.faceIndex

// Test if a sphere overlaps any triangle
const hits = [];
bvh.shapecast({
  intersectsBounds: (box) =>
    box.intersectsSphere(querySphere) ? INTERSECTED : NOT_INTERSECTED,
  intersectsTriangle: (tri, i) => { hits.push(i); }
});

// Clean up when done
geometry.disposeBoundsTree();

For large meshes, build the BVH in a WebWorker:

import { GenerateMeshBVHWorker } from 'three-mesh-bvh/src/workers/GenerateMeshBVHWorker.js';

const worker = new GenerateMeshBVHWorker();
const bvh = await worker.generate(geometry);
geometry.boundsTree = bvh;
// UI never freezes, even for million-triangle meshes

How much faster is BVH raycasting? The speedup grows with mesh complexity.

Without BVH (brute force)

Result: ~2 fps

With BVH (three-mesh-bvh)

1 ray × ~17 box + ~10 tri tests = ~27 tests
500 rays/frame = 13,500 tests/frame

Result: 60+ fps

Build cost vs. query savings

10K triangles → ~5ms build

Imperceptible. Just build it synchronously on load.

100K triangles → ~50ms build

One-frame stutter. Fine for loading screens, consider a worker for seamless loading.

1M triangles → ~500ms build

Use GenerateMeshBVHWorker. The main thread stays smooth.

A 50ms build that saves 16ms per frame pays for itself in 3 frames. After that, every frame runs faster for the lifetime of the mesh.

Where people use it

🖱️

Mouse picking

Click on any triangle in a 500K-polygon scene. Without BVH: impossible at 60fps. With BVH: trivial. Every 3D editor, configurator, and CAD viewer needs this.

🌊

SDF generation

To compute a signed distance field, you need the closest triangle to every grid point. With a 256³ grid and a 100K mesh, that's 1.6 trillion brute-force tests — or 268 million with BVH.

💡

Path tracing

three-gpu-pathtracer encodes the BVH into GPU textures and traverses it in fragment shaders. Real-time global illumination — in a browser.

✂️

CSG (Boolean operations)

Union, intersection, subtraction of meshes. three-bvh-csg uses dual-BVH traversal to find intersecting triangle pairs, then computes the boolean geometry.

🎮

Physics & collision

Character controllers need ground detection, wall sliding, and overlap tests every frame against arbitrary mesh colliders — not just convex hulls.

🖌️

Mesh painting & sculpting

Find all triangles under a brush radius. Sphere queries on the BVH return affected triangles in microseconds, enabling responsive 60fps sculpting.

History: from 1976 to today

1976

Clark introduces bounding volume hierarchies

James Clark proposes using hierarchical bounding volumes to accelerate visible-surface determination. The original BVH idea.

1984

Kay & Kajiya: slabs-based BVH for ray tracing

First practical use of BVH for ray-object intersection acceleration. Introduces the slab method for ray-box tests still used today.

1989

MacDonald & Booth: Surface Area Heuristic

The SAH is born — using surface area ratios to predict ray hit probabilities and choose optimal split positions.

2007

Wald: fast BVH construction on CPU

Techniques for building high-quality BVHs quickly enough for dynamic scenes. Brings SAH construction under 1 second for million-triangle scenes.

2012

Karras: parallel BVH on GPU

NVIDIA's approach using Morton codes and radix sort. Builds millions of primitives in milliseconds on the GPU.

2018

three-mesh-bvh v0.1.0

Garrett Johnson releases the first version. Initial focus: drop-in accelerated raycasting for web 3D.

2020

RTX hardware BVH traversal

NVIDIA RT cores perform BVH traversal in fixed-function silicon. 10 billion rays/second. BVH goes from software to hardware.

2022

three-mesh-bvh: shapecast, distance queries

The library grows beyond raycasting into a general-purpose spatial query engine.

2024

v0.7+: GPU path tracing integration

BVH encoded into textures for GLSL traversal. Powers three-gpu-pathtracer.

2025

v0.9: SkinnedMeshBVH, indirect mode

BVH for animated meshes with skeletal deformation. Indirect mode preserves the original index buffer.
