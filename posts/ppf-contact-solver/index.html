The problem: things pass through things

Open any video game. Grab a character's arm and swing it into a wall. Does the arm stop at the wall? Usually not. It clips right through. The hand pokes out the other side. Players call it "clipping." Developers call it "Tuesday."

In games, this is annoying but tolerable — you can fake your way past it with tricks. But in offline simulation for movies, fashion, and engineering, clipping is catastrophic:

👗

Virtual Fashion

ZOZO (Japan's largest fashion e-commerce) needs cloth to drape realistically on virtual bodies. A sleeve that passes through a torso is useless.

🎬

Movie VFX

When a character's cape billows, every fold must be physically correct. Intersection artifacts are visible at cinema resolution.

🏗️

Engineering

Simulating crash tests, fabric draping over furniture, or cable routing — penetration means wrong physics and wrong answers.

🤖

Robotics

Training robots to fold laundry or grasp deformable objects requires contact that never cheats.

THE CORE CHALLENGE

How do you simulate thousands of triangles falling, folding, and tangling — and guarantee that not a single triangle ever passes through another? That's the problem PPF Contact Solver solves.

Why contact is the hardest part of simulation

Simulating a bouncing ball is easy — it's just gravity and an elastic collision. Simulating a sheet of cloth falling onto a pile of objects is a nightmare. Here's why:

Collision detection at scale

A cloth with 10,000 triangles has ~50 million possible triangle pairs to check. Every timestep. At 100 steps per second. That's 5 billion checks per second — and most are useless because the triangles are far apart.

Tunneling

If your timestep is too large, a fast-moving thin cloth can teleport through an object between frames. One frame it's above, next frame it's below — collision detection never saw the intersection. This is called tunneling.

Stacking and friction

When cloth folds on itself, you get layers resting on layers resting on layers. Each layer pushes the one below, which pushes the next. The contact forces form a coupled system — you can't solve them independently.

Thin shells have no volume

A cloth triangle has zero thickness. Two triangles occupying the same plane aren't "intersecting" in the normal sense. You need a concept of distance between surfaces even when the gap is sub-millimeter.

Traditional approaches — penalty forces, constraint projection, impulse-based resolvers — all make compromises. Penalty forces let things overlap a little. Impulses can create jitter. Constraint methods can fail when too many contacts activate at once.

The breakthrough came from a line of research called Incremental Potential Contact (IPC), pioneered by Li et al. in 2020. PPF Contact Solver builds on this foundation with a new, faster barrier function.

Barrier functions — invisible force fields

Imagine you're walking toward a cliff edge. What stops you from falling off? Fear — a force that grows stronger the closer you get to the edge. At 10 meters, you barely notice. At 1 meter, you slow down. At 10 centimeters, you freeze.

A barrier function works the same way. It's a mathematical function that creates an invisible force field around every surface. When two surfaces approach each other:

THE BARRIER IDEA

The closer two surfaces get, the stronger the repulsive force. The force becomes infinite at zero distance — making penetration mathematically impossible. Not unlikely. Impossible.

Three barrier functions compared. As distance → 0, the barrier → ∞. The cubic barrier (PPF's innovation) rises steeply but stays smooth, making the solver faster and more stable.

The original IPC paper used a log barrier — the same kind used in interior-point methods for optimization. It works, but it has a problem: the stiffness (how hard the "force field" pushes) is separate from the material's own elasticity. This means the barrier and the elastic forces fight each other, requiring tiny timesteps to stay stable.

The cubic barrier: PPF's key insight

Ryoichi Ando's SIGGRAPH Asia 2024 paper — "A Cubic Barrier with Elasticity-Inclusive Dynamic Stiffness" — introduces two innovations:

📐

Cubic barrier shape

Instead of a logarithmic curve, the barrier uses a cubic polynomial. It's zero beyond a threshold distance d̂, rises as a smooth cubic as surfaces approach, and still goes to infinity at zero distance. The cubic shape means simpler derivatives — faster to compute on GPU.

🔗

Elasticity-inclusive stiffness

The barrier's stiffness adapts to the material. Stiff steel gets a stiff barrier. Soft silk gets a soft barrier. This eliminates the tug-of-war between contact forces and elastic forces, allowing much larger timesteps.

Think of it like this. The original IPC barrier is like putting the same steel bumper on every car — a Smart car and a tank get the same bumper. PPF's barrier matches the bumper to the car: a soft foam bumper for the Smart car, an armored plate for the tank.

THE MATH (SIMPLIFIED)

The cubic barrier function looks like:

b(d) = (d − d̂)³ / d   when d < d̂,   0 otherwise

Where d is the distance between two surfaces and d̂ is the activation threshold. The key property: as d → 0, b(d) → ∞. Penetration requires infinite energy — it simply can't happen.

FEM in 30 seconds

PPF simulates materials using the Finite Element Method (FEM). If you've never heard of FEM, here's the 30-second version:

A mesh of triangles. Each triangle is a "finite element" — a small patch of material with known physics. FEM computes how each element deforms under forces, then stitches the results together. Drag to orbit.
Divide the object into elements

A cloth becomes a mesh of triangles. A solid becomes a mesh of tetrahedra. Each element is a tiny piece of material with its own stress and strain.

Write physics equations for each element

Each triangle knows its rest shape. When it deforms, it resists — like a rubber band. The resistance follows a constitutive model (the "material law"). PPF uses a Neo-Hookean model for realistic nonlinear elasticity.

Assemble and solve

Stitch all the element equations into one giant system. Solve for the new positions of every vertex. This is where the GPU shines — the system is sparse and highly parallelizable.

KEY INSIGHT

PPF computes symbolic force Jacobians — the derivatives of forces with respect to positions are derived analytically, not numerically approximated. This gives exact gradients, which makes the Newton solver converge in fewer iterations.

How PPF puts it all together

Here's the simulation loop that runs every timestep. Each component is massively parallel on the GPU:

1

Broad-phase collision detection

Using spatial hashing on the GPU, quickly find pairs of triangles that might be close. This prunes billions of impossible pairs down to thousands of candidates.

2

Narrow-phase distance computation

For each candidate pair, compute the exact distance between the triangle surfaces. PPF handles point-triangle, edge-edge, and point-edge distances.

3

Barrier energy + Elastic energy

Sum up: (a) the cubic barrier energy from all close contacts, and (b) the FEM elastic energy from all deformed triangles. The total is one big energy function.

4

Newton's method

Minimize the total energy using Newton's method with line search. The Jacobians are exact (symbolic), so convergence is fast. The line search includes a continuous collision detection (CCD) check to ensure no triangle tunnels through another during the step.

5

Strain limiting

After the solve, check that no triangle has stretched beyond a configurable bound (e.g., 1%). If it has, project it back. This keeps cloth from turning into silly putty.

The entire pipeline runs in single precision (FP32) on the GPU. No double precision needed — the cubic barrier's smooth derivatives keep things numerically stable even in 32-bit float.

PPF Contact Solver isn't a toy demo. It's a production engine that handles scenarios most solvers choke on:

🪢 Twisted ropes

Rod-rod contact with hundreds of self-contacts per frame. Ropes that knot, tangle, and untangle.

👚 Layered garments

Multiple cloth layers (shirt, jacket, scarf) interacting simultaneously. Each layer contacts the body and every other layer.

🪵 Solid-cloth coupling

Rigid or deformable solid objects interacting with thin shells. Buttons through buttonholes, zippers, clasps.

📐 Strict strain limits

Triangles never stretch beyond 1%. Real fabric doesn't stretch like rubber — PPF enforces this strictly.

The history: how we got here

1998

Baraff & Witkin — Implicit cloth

The paper that made cloth simulation practical. Implicit integration allowed large timesteps. But contact handling was ad-hoc.

2002–2012

Penalty forces & impulse methods

Approximate contact by adding spring-like penalty forces when things get close. Fast but leaky — things still penetrate if forces aren't strong enough.

2020

IPC — Incremental Potential Contact

Li et al. introduce the barrier function approach. Penetration-free guarantee. Game changer — but slow for stiff materials due to barrier-elasticity conflict.

2024

PPF — Cubic Barrier (Ando, SIGGRAPH Asia)

Elasticity-inclusive stiffness eliminates the barrier-elasticity conflict. Cubic shape is GPU-friendly. Handles 180M+ contacts. Open-sourced by ZOZO.
