1. Nature already solved this

Bone is one of the most efficient structures in the natural world. A femur cross-section looks chaotic at first glance -- a lattice of fine struts called trabeculae, packed more densely near the outer shell, sparse in the middle. That is not randomness. It is millions of years of material placed exactly where force flows.

Topology optimization removes material from where it does not carry load, mimicking how bone adapts to stress.

This is the core idea of topology optimization: given a fixed region of space, a set of forces, and a material budget, find the distribution of material that makes the structure as stiff as possible. The algorithm grows structure the way bone does -- by killing off underloaded material and reinforcing the rest.

2. What topology optimization actually is

There are three levels of structural optimization. They are often confused:

↔

Size optimization

Shape is fixed. Tune numbers: beam thickness, spring constant, plate gauge. The design space is a handful of parameters.

△

Shape optimization

Topology is fixed. Move boundary curves to smooth stress concentrations. Think filleting a corner. Adds thousands of parameters (node positions).

⬢

Topology optimization

Nothing fixed. The algorithm decides where material exists at all -- holes can appear, branches can merge, arches can form from a blank canvas.

🧰

Generative design

Industry marketing term for topology optimization + multiple load cases + manufacturing constraints. Same math, fancier UI (Fusion 360, nTopology).

Topology optimization is the hardest and most powerful level. The design space is every possible hole pattern inside a domain -- combinatorially infinite. The algorithm we will build, called SIMP, sidesteps the combinatorics with a continuous relaxation trick.

THE PROBLEM

Maximize stiffness of a structure subject to: (1) force equilibrium (FEA), (2) a material budget (volume fraction). Control variable: which cells contain material.

3. The finite element foundation

Before we can optimize, we need to measure stiffness. That is what finite element analysis (FEA) does: it discretizes the domain into small cells, assembles a global stiffness matrix, and solves for displacements under the applied loads.

Each cell in the FE mesh carries a density rho between 0 (void) and 1 (solid). FEA solves for displacements at every node given those densities.

Each element contributes to the global stiffness matrix K proportionally to how much material it contains. The system to solve is simply:

EQUILIBRIUM
K(x) u = F

where x is the vector of element densities, u is displacement, and F is the applied load. Compliance (the inverse of stiffness -- lower is stiffer) is then:

COMPLIANCE (OBJECTIVE)
C(x) = F^T u = u^T K(x) u

We want to minimize C subject to the volume constraint. That is the whole optimization problem in two lines of math.

4. SIMP -- density as a dial

The real design space is binary: each cell is either solid (1) or void (0). Searching that space directly is NP-hard for any reasonable mesh. The SIMP trick (Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization) relaxes the problem: allow densities to be continuous between 0 and 1, but penalize the grey zone.

SIMP MATERIAL MODEL
E(rho) = E_min + rho^p * (E_0 - E_min) , p = 3

The exponent p = 3 is the key. At rho = 0.5, the element only contributes 0.5^3 = 12.5% of full stiffness, but costs 50% of the material budget. The optimizer learns that grey cells are expensive for little structural gain -- so it pushes densities toward 0 or 1 on its own.

The p=3 exponent creates a strong penalty for intermediate densities. Grey cells get little stiffness for their material cost, so the optimizer eliminates them.

5. Sensitivity: where does stiffness care about material?

To minimize compliance we need its gradient with respect to each element density: "if I add a tiny bit of material to element e, how much does compliance change?" This is the sensitivity, and it has a beautiful closed form.

COMPLIANCE SENSITIVITY (chain rule)
dC/dxe = -p * xe^(p-1) * (E_0 - E_min) * ue^T * KE * ue

Read this as: the sensitivity is large where the element's local strain energy ue^T KE ue is large. High strain energy = this element is working hard. We want MORE material where elements work hard, and less where they are idle.

KEY INSIGHT

You only need the displacement field u (from one FEA solve) to compute the sensitivity for ALL elements simultaneously. One solve per iteration, not one per element.

1

Assemble K(x)

Build global stiffness from current densities using SIMP material model.

2

Solve K u = F

One linear system solve. Direct (numpy) for small meshes; conjugate gradient for large.

3

Compute sensitivities

Loop all elements: extract ue, compute ue^T KE ue. Multiply by penalized exponent.

4

Filter sensitivities

Average each sensitivity with neighbors using cone-shaped weight. Removes checkerboard.

5

Update densities (OC)

Bisect on Lagrange multiplier to satisfy volume constraint. Multiplicative update per element.

6. The density filter -- smoothing away checkerboard

Without regularization, SIMP produces a checkerboard: alternating solid and void cells that is stiff on paper but completely unprintable. The fix: replace each sensitivity with a weighted average of its neighbors.

SENSITIVITY FILTER
dc_hat_e = (1/(x_e * sum_f H_ef)) * sum_f H_ef * x_f * dc_f
H_ef = max(0, R_min - dist(e,f))

R_min (filter radius) controls minimum feature size. Larger R_min = smoother result with thicker members. Typical values: 1.5 to 2.5 element lengths.

7. Optimality Criteria -- a fast update rule

The Optimality Criteria (OC) method exploits the KKT structure of the volume-constrained problem. Instead of gradient descent, it uses a multiplicative update anchored by a Lagrange multiplier found via bisection.

OC DENSITY UPDATE
x_new_e = clip( x_e * sqrt(-dc_e / (lambda * dV_e)), x_e-move, x_e+move )

Lambda enforces the volume budget. The move limit (typically 0.2) prevents any element from changing too fast. OC converges in 30-80 iterations while gradient descent takes 200+.

WHY OC BEATS GRADIENT DESCENT

OC is multiplicative -- it naturally moves low-density elements slowly and high-density elements faster. It automatically satisfies the volume constraint via bisection instead of requiring a penalty term.

9. Real systems: from research to industry

The 99-line SIMP we just built is the research baseline. Production tools extend it in several critical directions:

// academic SIMP

  • 2D structured mesh
  • Direct dense solver (numpy)
  • One load case
  • No manufacturing constraints
  • Sensitivity filter only
  • Cantilever or MBB beam tests

// production tools

  • 3D tetrahedral meshes from CAD
  • Iterative multigrid solver (GPU)
  • Many load cases (fatigue, crash)
  • Overhang, symmetry, extrusion constraints
  • Heaviside projection for sharp boundaries
  • Full car bodies, aerospace brackets
⛸

Altair OptiStruct

The industry standard. Used by automotive OEMs (BMW, Ford, Toyota) for chassis and bracket optimization. SIMP core with many manufacturing constraints.

🛠

nTopology

Cloud-native implicit modeling + topology opt. Field-driven design means you can apply different lattice densities based on stress fields.

💾

Autodesk Fusion 360

Generative design UI on top of topology opt. Accessible to mechanical engineers without FEA expertise. Exports directly to CAM.

🔬

Research frontiers

Level-set methods (sharp boundaries), BESO (evolutionary removal), neural topology opt (NTopo), differentiable FEM (JAX-FEM).

10. Historical context

1904

Michell Trusses

A.G.M. Michell derives the theoretical minimum-weight truss layouts analytically. The resulting spiderweb-like patterns are still the benchmark for TO results 120 years later.

1988

Bendsoe and Kikuchi

First computational topology optimization using homogenization -- treating cells as microstructured composites. Works but slow; needs design interpretation step.

1999

SIMP -- Bendsoe and Sigmund

The power-law material model (solid isotropic material with penalization). Simple, fast, provably convergent. Becomes the dominant method overnight.

2001

Sigmund 99-line code

Ole Sigmund publishes a complete SIMP implementation in 99 lines of MATLAB. It is downloaded millions of times and becomes the de-facto tutorial entry point worldwide.

2010

Level-set methods mature

Allaire, Jouve, Toader et al. develop level-set topology optimization. Produces smooth, CAD-ready boundaries -- critical for additive manufacturing workflows.

2018

GPU-accelerated TO

Large-scale problems (100M+ elements) become tractable on GPU via matrix-free conjugate gradient solvers with multigrid preconditioning. Real car bodies in hours.

2023+

Neural topology optimization

Networks trained on thousands of SIMP solutions can predict near-optimal layouts in milliseconds. NTopo, TopoDiff, and similar models enable real-time design exploration.
