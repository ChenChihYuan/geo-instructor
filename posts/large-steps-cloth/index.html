The bottleneck: tiny timesteps

It's 1997. You want to animate a flag waving in the wind for a movie. You set up your cloth simulator — a mesh of triangles, springs connecting vertices, gravity pulling everything down. You hit "Run."

And you wait. And wait. And wait.

The problem isn't that cloth physics is complicated. The problem is that your simulator is forced to take microscopic timesteps. A single second of animation might require 10,000 steps. Each step solves forces, updates velocities, moves vertices. At 10,000 steps per second of animation, a 30-second cloth sequence means 300,000 integration steps.

THE BOTTLENECK

Before 1998, cloth simulators used explicit integration (forward Euler, Runge-Kutta). These methods are simple but demand tiny timesteps to stay stable. Make the step too big and the simulation explodes — springs overshoot, energy grows without bound, cloth flies apart.

Baraff and Witkin asked: what if instead of taking 10,000 tiny steps per second, we could take 30 large steps? Same physics, same result, 300× less work.

Why explicit methods crawl

To understand the breakthrough, you need to understand the disease. Let's start with the simplest possible "cloth" — a single mass on a spring.

TOY MODEL

A mass hangs from a spring. Gravity pulls it down. The spring pulls it back up. The mass oscillates. This is the simplest dynamical system, and it already contains the instability problem.

Forward Euler (explicit) works like this: 

v_new = v_old + (F / m) * dt
x_new = x_old + v_new * dt

Simple. Intuitive. You compute the force now, then step forward. But there's a trap: if dt is too large relative to the spring's stiffness, the mass overshoots the equilibrium point. Next step, the spring pulls it back even harder. It overshoots further. Energy grows. The system explodes.

The stability limit for forward Euler on a spring with stiffness k and mass m is:

dt < 2 · √(m / k)

Cloth has springs with very different stiffnesses. Stretch resistance is high (fabric doesn't stretch much). Bend resistance is low (fabric folds easily). The stiffest spring dictates the timestep for the entire system. A stiff stretch spring might force dt below 0.0001 seconds — even though the bending motion could tolerate dt = 0.01.

💥

The explosion

Step too big → spring overshoots → energy grows → vertices fly to infinity. This is numerical instability, and it's the #1 enemy of cloth simulation.

🐌

The crawl

Step small enough → stable, but painfully slow. A 10-second cloth draping might take hours of compute. The stiffest spring holds the entire simulation hostage.

The implicit leap

Baraff and Witkin's key idea: instead of computing forces at the current position and stepping forward (explicit), compute forces at the future position (implicit).

Backward Euler (implicit) says: 

v_new = v_old + (F(x_new) / m) * dt
x_new = x_old + v_new * dt

Wait — F(x_new) depends on x_new, which is what we're trying to compute! This is a chicken-and-egg problem. To find the new position, we need the force at the new position, but to compute that force, we need the new position.

The solution: linearize. Approximate the force at the new position using a first-order Taylor expansion:

F(x₀ + Δx) ≈ F(x₀) + (∂F/∂x) · Δx

The term ∂F/∂x is the Jacobian of the force — how the force changes as position changes. For a spring, this is just the negative stiffness: ∂F/∂x = −k.

THE KEY INSIGHT

Implicit Euler turns the time-stepping problem into a linear system:

(M − dt² · ∂F/∂x) · Δv = dt · F(x₀)

This is A · x = b. Solving a linear system is more expensive per step than forward Euler, but the system is unconditionally stable — you can take timesteps 50× to 100× larger. The net speedup is enormous.

Why is it stable? Intuitively, because the force evaluation already "knows" about the future. If the mass would overshoot, the linearized future force acts as a brake. The stiffer the spring, the stronger the brake. Stiff springs, which were the enemy of explicit methods, become self-correcting under implicit integration.

Cloth forces — stretch, shear, bend

Baraff and Witkin model cloth as a triangle mesh. Each triangle resists three kinds of deformation:

Stretch

Pulling the fabric along its warp or weft direction. Real cloth barely stretches — cotton extends less than 2% before tearing. The stretch condition measures how much each triangle has elongated relative to its rest shape. High stiffness here is what makes explicit methods suffer.

Shear

Distorting the fabric diagonally — turning a square into a parallelogram. The shear condition measures the angle change between the warp and weft directions within each triangle.

Bend

Folding along an edge shared by two triangles. The bend condition measures the dihedral angle between adjacent triangles. Bend stiffness is much lower than stretch — that's why cloth folds instead of stretching.

Each condition produces a scalar energy that depends on vertex positions. The force is the negative gradient: F = −∂E/∂x. The force Jacobian — needed for implicit integration — is ∂F/∂x = −∂²E/∂x², the Hessian of the energy.

Baraff and Witkin derive these analytically using a continuum formulation based on condition functions C(x). The energy is E = ½ k C(x)², giving forces that are linear in the condition and Jacobians that are constant matrices (for stretch/shear). This makes the linear system cheap to assemble.

The linear system

At each timestep, the simulator assembles and solves:

(M − dt · ∂f/∂v − dt² · ∂f/∂x) · Δv = dt · (f₀ + dt · (∂f/∂x) · v₀)

Where M is the mass matrix (diagonal — each vertex has a mass), f₀ are forces at the current state, ∂f/∂x and ∂f/∂v are force Jacobians with respect to position and velocity.

This is a sparse linear system. Each vertex only interacts with its immediate neighbors through the triangle mesh. A cloth with 10,000 vertices produces a 30,000 × 30,000 matrix (3 DOF per vertex), but it's mostly zeros.

WHY CONJUGATE GRADIENT?

Baraff and Witkin solve this system using the modified conjugate gradient (CG) method. CG is iterative — it doesn't need the full matrix, just the ability to multiply the matrix by a vector. For sparse systems, this is much cheaper than direct factorization. They typically need only 10–30 CG iterations per timestep.

1

Assemble forces f₀

Loop over all triangles. Compute stretch, shear, bend conditions. Accumulate forces at each vertex.

2

Assemble Jacobians ∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂v

For each triangle, compute the 9×9 (or 12×12 for bending) block of the Jacobian. Scatter into the global sparse matrix.

3

Build the system matrix A and right-hand side b

A = M − dt·∂f/∂v − dt²·∂f/∂x. b = dt·(f₀ + dt·∂f/∂x·v₀). Both are sparse.

4

Solve A·Δv = b via modified CG

10–30 iterations. Constraints are enforced within the CG loop by filtering the search direction (more on this below).

5

Update state

v_new = v_old + Δv, x_new = x_old + dt·v_new. One large step, done.

Constraints — pinning without cheating

Cloth draping over a body needs some vertices pinned — fixed at a shoulder, or constrained to slide along a surface. Traditional approaches apply massive penalty springs to keep vertices in place. But penalty springs are stiff, which defeats the purpose of implicit integration.

Baraff and Witkin embed constraints directly into the CG solver. The idea is elegant: at each CG iteration, project the search direction to zero out any motion along constrained directions. A pinned vertex contributes zero to the velocity update, exactly, regardless of how many CG iterations run.

CONSTRAINT PROJECTION

For each constrained particle i, define a projection matrix Pᵢ that zeroes out the constrained components of velocity. During CG iteration, filter: z = P · r (preconditioned residual) and Δv = P · Δv (velocity update). The constraints are satisfied exactly, every iteration.

This is far more elegant than penalty springs. No extra stiffness in the system. No fighting between constraint forces and cloth forces. The constraint is algebraic, not physical.

Why it matters — the legacy

This paper didn't just make cloth simulation faster. It changed the culture of physics simulation in graphics. Before Baraff & Witkin, most graphics researchers avoided implicit methods — they seemed complicated, the matrix assembly was daunting, and explicit methods were "good enough" for simple scenes.

After this paper, implicit integration became the default for deformable simulation. Every modern cloth engine — from Pixar's Fizt (which Baraff and Witkin themselves built) to Weta's tissue system to Houdini's Vellum — traces its lineage back to this work.

The timeline: before and after

1987

Terzopoulos et al. — Deformable models

First physics-based cloth in graphics. Finite differences on a grid. Slow but groundbreaking.

1992

Carignan et al. — Garment simulation

Cloth on virtual mannequins. Explicit integration. Small timesteps, limited to simple garments.

1994

Breen et al. — Particle-based draping

Interacting particles model woven structure. Beautiful results, very slow.

1998

Baraff & Witkin — Large Steps ★

Implicit integration + modified CG + constraint filtering. 50–100× speedup. The paper that made cloth practical for production.

2002

Bridson et al. — Robust collision handling

Added proper collision detection and response to implicit cloth. Combined with Baraff-Witkin, this became the standard pipeline.

2003–2010

Production adoption

Pixar (Fizt/Ariel), Weta, ILM all build implicit cloth solvers based on this foundation. Every movie with capes, dresses, or flags uses descendants of this paper.

2016

Macklin et al. — XPBD

Position-Based Dynamics with XPBD corrections brings implicit-like stability to real-time games. Different approach, same goal: large stable steps.

2020

Li et al. — IPC

Incremental Potential Contact: guaranteed penetration-free simulation. Builds on the implicit integration foundation Baraff & Witkin established.
